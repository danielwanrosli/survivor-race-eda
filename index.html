Exploring Contestant Performance in Survivor Before and After the CBS Diversity Promise

In 2020, CBS pledged that all reality casts will be represented with at least 50% people of color. In this article, we will be looking at the policy's impact on the show, Survivor. Survivor is a microcosm of society in that it highlights the need to strategize, form alliances, and manipulate one another in order to succeed in a competitive environment. Historically, POC contestants have been naturally subject to early elimination due to innate similar-attraction being a large reason as to why dominant alliances form. In my article, I would like to analyze whether CBS’ new policy has had its intended effect in giving people of color an equal opportunity to succeed, and consider the nuances of how gender and ethnicity may play a deeper role in a contestant’s success.

Introduction

Survivor is a reality TV show where contestants compete against each other for a million dollar prize. Every few days at "Tribal Council", the players must vote off one of the players from their tribe, eliminating them from the game. Contestants are inclined to form alliances and manipulate each other to leverage their position in the game. The game is divided into a team portion known as the "pre-merge" where the team that loses the immunity challenge is sent to Tribal Council. Contestants then enter the "merge" where the tribes are combined into one tribe and the game becomes individual. The game concludes with a final Tribal Council, where the remaining players must make their case to a jury comprised of their eliminated peers who decide the Sole Survivor by majority vote.

The 20-year long trend of casting predominantly White casts on Survivor has had a disproportionate effect on the outcomes of the show's winners. Not only is this an inaccurate representation of the demographics of the U.S, it is indicative of a larger issue in the media––people of color are largely underrepresented in American media. With the show striving for diversity, we will analyze whether or not the playing field was leveled for all contestants to have a fair opportunity.

As a huge Survivor fan and someone who was generally aware of the lack of diversity the show had, seeing how dominantly White the winner’s circle was in each era was still astonishing. Since the diversity initiative, there have only been three complete seasons, so this sample size is not nearly equivalent to the sizes of the other samples. However, it is still notable that of the three seasons produced after the diversity initiative, we have immediately produced two POC winners. Additionally, Season 42 saw a black woman winning Survivor for the first time in nearly 20 years, with the last black woman winning in Season 4. It is also interesting to note that seasons 13 and 14 played around with the idea of a cast that was evenly split between the four races being compared in the article. Both seasons produced a POC winner, where season 13 produced a final three where ⅔ were Asian, and season 14 produced an all-Black final three. These are additional data points that support innate-similar attraction being a large reason as to why alliances form, and why so many seasons that lack people of color are White dominated.

For our next visualization, we will be taking the performance of every POC contestant per season, averaging their placement, and taking the percentile over the number of castmates to create a measurement of evaluating the success of POC contestants. We will be comparing this among the ratio of POC contestants per season to evaluate to what extent the success of POC contestants is impacted by the number of POC cast.

Within the visualization, we can evaluate the average success percentile above 50% as a positive performance, and below 50% as a negative performance. As seen by the chart, 8/40 of the seasons prior to the diversity policy resulted in a negative performance by POC contestants. Meanwhile, of the three seasons that took place after the diversity policy, two seasons (41 and 42) resulted in a positive performance. More notably, these two seasons had POC winners, while season 43 had a White winner. Another interesting thing to note is that seasons 13 and 14 had an almost even split of White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic/Latino contestants cast. While both seasons resulted in a POC winner, season 13 had a negative performance by POC contestants, and season 14 had a positive performance by POC contestants. Season 13 had a final three represented by two Asian contestants and one Latino contestant, while season 14 had a final three represented by three Black contestants. This is another data point that supports the idea that innate-similar attraction is a large reason as to why alliances form, and why so many seasons that lack people of color are White dominated.

As seen in this visualization, there are five seasons that have a particularly negative performance by POC contestants and fall below or at the 30th percentile. These seasons are 6, 10, 28, 33, and 37. In our final visualzation, we will be using these five seasons to find a pattern in how gender plays a role in contestant performance by analyzing several rates of elimination in the pre-merge phase. Being indicated in the pre-merge is often indicated as a poor performance, due to not making it to the individual portion of the game and generally resulting in a bottom-half placement. Because this portion of this game is revolved around team challenges, however, contestants often prioritize who they keep based on perceived strength. Oftentimes, women disproportionately do not fit into this archetype. In our final visualization, we will how gender and race may influence early exit in the game due to the nature of this stage of the game.

Conclusion

The diversity initiative has had spectacular results in leveling the playing field, with two POC female winners in Seasons 41 and 42. Yet approximately half of the POC women were eliminated before the merge in two of the three post-policy seasons. Despite this, the initiative has generally been seen as successful in increasing the success of minority contestants.

Additionally, the diversity initiative in Survivor is a great example for other media platforms to emulate. By encouraging diversity, Survivor has been successful in increasing the success of minority contestants and providing a platform for their stories to be told. I hope for Survivor and similar reality shows to continue striving towards creating an equitable and representative society within their shows.
